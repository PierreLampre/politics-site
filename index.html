1) What are fire districts?

Fire districts are separate local government entities that exist to fund and govern fire protection in a defined area. In practice, they often feel invisible ‚Äî but they can levy taxes, manage budgets, oversee equipment, and run elections for commissioners.

Click to expand: The important part people miss

Fire districts are not automatic. They were created because residents petitioned for them ‚Äî meaning the underlying model assumes the public wanted an active role in fire protection governance and oversight.

That premise matters, because if the public stops participating, the structure becomes ‚Äúgovernment by procedure‚Äù instead of ‚Äúgovernment by consent.‚Äù

Click to expand: What do commissioners actually do?
  • Approve budgets and tax levy decisions related to fire protection
  • Oversee contracts, purchases, audits, compliance, and public notices
  • Help set direction for how services are staffed and supported (volunteer, paid, hybrid realities)
  • Maintain continuity and decision-making for essential emergency readiness

2) Why the elections matter (and why turnout matters more)

Fire district elections are the public‚Äôs mechanism to choose who sits on these boards. The structural problem is simple: turnout is routinely below 2% of registered voters.

Reality check When participation is that low, elections stop functioning as meaningful consent.

Even if everything is being run honestly, the system becomes hard to defend: who is it really accountable to?

Click to expand: The uncomfortable question

If almost nobody participates year after year, it naturally raises a bigger question:

‚ÄúWhy do these entities still exist?‚Äù

That question is not a rhetorical dunk. It‚Äôs a governance question ‚Äî and it should be answered with evidence, not assumptions.

Click to expand: My working theory (and a fair counterpoint)

Working theory: the issue is not just ‚Äúapathy.‚Äù It‚Äôs also design and communication failure ‚Äî broken links, unclear schedules, poor visibility, and a culture that assumes nobody shows up.

Fair counterpoint: some districts may function well with a small but consistent core of engaged stakeholders (often firefighters), and they may fear larger electorates would vote without understanding operational realities.

Both can be true ‚Äî which is why participation + documentation is the only honest stress test.

Given the timeline, the more reasonable approach for this cycle is to treat these like serious elections:

  • Encourage participation and voter awareness
  • Run meet & greets / candidate forums / interviews
  • Write short explainer articles and share meeting info
  • Document who runs, who votes, and what changes (if any)

Then, after the election, we can evaluate the results and ask bigger structural questions with actual evidence.

Article 1: Why I interviewed a fire district secretary ‚Äî and what the State Comptroller reports reveal

Fire district elections are easy to miss. They are not dramatic. They are not flashy. And they are rarely talked about. Yet they are legally required elections that directly affect local taxes, emergency services, public assets, and millions of dollars in public spending. They operate under a statutory framework that assumes public participation ‚Äî but in practice, very little awareness, entry, or sustained oversight seems to exist. This article explains why I have an interview scheduled next Tuesday with Kaycy Catalano, a fire district secretary, and why that conversation matters ‚Äî not just locally, but structurally. Next Tuesday, December 30th, 92.1 WVLT, 5-6pm. https://www.facebook.com/wvltradio Why this coverage exists at all For the past 2.5 years, my work through InformTheVoteNJ.com has partially focused on identifying where local election systems have gaps ‚Äî not rhetorically, but operationally. Fire district elections are obscure. They are a known low-participation area of New Jersey governance, documented by the New Jersey Office of the State Comptroller in a 2014 audit and a subsequent follow-up report. https://www.nj.gov/.../report_fire_districts_05_28_2014.pdf https://www.nj.gov/.../fire_districtsfollowup_audit... That audit examined only three fire districts out of roughly 185 statewide ‚Äî a small sample. But its relevance lies not in the number of districts audited, but in the conditions under which problems emerged: off-cycle February elections, extremely low voter turnout, minimal public attendance at meetings, limited press scrutiny. Those same conditions are observable in Cumberland County today. I was literally told at my first meeting that I was the second person from the public to attend in 12 years. To be clear, the audit did not claim that every fire district is mismanaged. It demonstrated what can occur when elected governance operates with little meaningful public participation. What the Comptroller actually found ‚Äî and why it matters The Comptroller documented voter turnout below 2 percent in all three audited districts ‚Äî even when voters were approving budgets ranging from $2 million to $25 million. This is not a marginal concern. At that level of participation: elections cease to function as mechanisms of public consent, accountability becomes theoretical, and governance relies on procedure rather than validation. Importantly, the Comptroller‚Äôs findings were debated by the districts themselves. Cherry Hill and Woodbridge pushed back on comparisons, compensation analysis, and operational assumptions. That disagreement matters, because it proves the issues are legitimately debatable, not settled facts. What was not debated was voter turnout. Cherry Hill explicitly acknowledged it as a problem. Woodbridge acknowledged the numbers but opposed moving elections to November as the Comptroller recommended, and despite legislation allowing it, preferring status quo stability over reform. That contrast reveals something essential: Disagreements over fire district practices fall within ordinary cost‚Äìbenefit analysis, where elected boards exercise authority to make final decisions. The structural concern arises earlier: whether elections with consistently negligible participation provide sufficient democratic grounding for that authority in the first place. Audits without public oversight have limits The Comptroller‚Äôs follow-up report shows that districts largely implemented fiscal and operational recommendations. But the most democracy-reinforcing recommendation ‚Äî moving elections to November to increase turnout ‚Äî was discretionary. Only one of three districts adopted it. Six years later, the system still allows fire districts to operate with: low turnout, limited public scrutiny, and elections that function more as procedural clearance than democratic choice. Audits can improve internal practices. They cannot substitute for public oversight. That distinction matters deeply for counties like Cumberland, where no comparable audit appears to exist, where similar participation patterns are already visible, and previously drafted bills by then State Senators Beach, Turner, and Van Drew tried to push in the direction of higher voter turnout. https://www.troysingleton.com/bid_to_hike_2_voter_turnout... https://pub.njleg.gov/bills/2016/S2500/2002_I1.HTM The volunteer question ‚Äî and the false simplicity of ‚Äúcost savings‚Äù The Comptroller also examined volunteer versus paid firefighter models, often framed as a cost-saving opportunity. Cherry Hill‚Äôs follow-up response is instructive. After evaluation, it rejected expanding volunteer firefighting, citing: training requirements (over 200 hours annually), call volume, population growth, ISO rating standards, retention challenges, and lack of mutual aid capacity. This complicates a common narrative. Fire protection is essential, dangerous, and expected to function flawlessly ‚Äî even though much of that readiness is invisible and involves long periods of waiting. The issue is not whether firefighters should be busy every moment. The issue is whether communities are clear about the service model they are choosing. What becomes problematic is expecting: professional, reliable emergency services while funding and governing them as if unpaid labor can fill the gap without meaningful public involvement in defining those tradeoffs. Fire districts are optional ‚Äî which makes participation essential Under New Jersey law (N.J.S.A. 40A:14-70), municipalities do not have to create fire districts. They exist because residents petitioned for them ‚Äî a deliberate choice to treat fire protection as a publicly governed service with elections, budgets, and voter oversight. That structure only makes sense if the public remains meaningfully involved. When turnout is persistently low, meetings are sparsely attended, and candidacy itself is difficult to sustain, the foundational premise of the model weakens. At that point, a basic question emerges: If the public is absent from the governance of an essential service, what purpose are these elections actually serving? The election timeline ‚Äî and the illusion of ‚Äúearly‚Äù coverage February fire district elections follow a compressed schedule: Late December: mail-in ballot eligibility begins January 10: commissioners must publish notice of the election (obvious through-line county-wide question: Where again are these required to be published?) January 23: nomination petitions are due February 21: Election Day None of these dates are controversial. What is concerning is how little visible time they allow for: candidate recruitment, public understanding, and meaningful deliberation before ballots are cast. Claims that coverage is ‚Äúearly‚Äù don‚Äôt hold up under this timeline. By early January, most of the process is already underway. How much time is there ‚Äî really? Across Cumberland County, there are roughly eight fire district elections, each with one or two seats potentially open. Under healthy participation, that might mean, at minimum, a dozen candidates. If that level of engagement materialized Friday (since I'm not going to bother people on Christmas Eve or Christmas Day) ‚Äî with basic cooperation from candidates and officials ‚Äî meaningful coverage would be possible. Which shifts the question: If coverage and engagement are feasible, why do they so rarely occur? After years of observation, a pattern emerges: the constraint is often not public apathy, but institutional design and cultural expectations that assume disengagement. Why this interview, and why now That‚Äôs why I‚Äôm interviewing Kaycy Catalano next Tuesday. Among fire district officials I‚Äôve encountered, Kaycy has been notably communicative ‚Äî willing to explain processes, acknowledge challenges, and discuss voter participation candidly. This interview is not about assigning blame. It‚Äôs about understanding: how the system is supposed to function, where it has gaps, and whether meaningful participation is realistically supported. At the time of publishing this draft, I am operating under reasonable assumptions: I will begin further outreach on Friday using a list of contacts obtained via OPRA request. Eight fire district elections likely cost county taxpayers at least a hundred thousand dollars to administer, if not more. I'll see if I can get official projected costs after Christmas. Based on statewide patterns and local observation, turnout is likely to remain under 2 percent without structural change. At one meeting I was told their district gets about 20 votes per year. What I‚Äôll be watching next As February approaches, I‚Äôll be tracking: where and how election notices are published, whether candidate recruitment becomes visible, when candidacies are disclosed, whether required meetings occur and are documented (I have probable cause for this), and how clearly voters are informed about budgets and authority. Where information exists, it will be shared. Where it does not, that absence will be documented. Closing thought Democracy is not validated by the existence of elections, but by the role those elections actually play. When elections that approve multi-million-dollar budgets routinely attract fewer than 2 percent of eligible voters, the issue is not necessarily public apathy ‚Äî it is probably also institutional design. Fire district are easy to ignore because the system surrounding them provides very little space for awareness, discussion, or entry. Whether that is acceptable is not a rhetorical question. It‚Äôs one that deserves to be examined ‚Äî openly, calmly, and with evidence.

Make FireFighting Sexy Again (Not Just a Marketing Ploy) https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=APIHorR0oYs (interview link) What I Learned, What‚Äôs at Stake, and Why Participation Is the Only Honest Stress Test From My Interview with Kacy Catalano For the past few weeks, I‚Äôve been poking my head into Cumberland County fire districts ‚Äî reading audits, attending meetings, tracking election timelines, and asking what feels like an increasingly unavoidable question: What is the point of an election that almost nobody participates in? That question was the backbone of a piece I published ahead of my recent radio interview with Kacy Catalano, Fire District Secretary for Commercial Township Fire District #3 (Laurel Lake). That article laid out the structural concern: fire districts are optional, tax-levying government entities that operate under a framework that assumes public participation ‚Äî even though, in practice, turnout is routinely under 2 percent. Now that the interview has happened, the question is even less abstract. We now have a clearer picture of what fire district governance actually looks like from the inside ‚Äî how much work it requires, how little visibility it receives, and how heavily it depends on a very small number of people quietly carrying the load. And most importantly, we now have a near-term decision point: the February fire district elections are approaching. Given the reality of the timeline, the most direct and honest path forward is no longer theoretical reform. It is awareness, participation, and stress-testing the system as it currently exists. What the interview clarified ‚Äî beyond the audits The State Comptroller‚Äôs reports (2014 audit and follow-up) documented conditions that many people, including myself, suspected but could not easily verify locally: February off-cycle elections Voter turnout below 2 percent Minimal public attendance at meetings Little to no press coverage What the interview with Kacy Catalano added was texture. Not just how fire districts are supposed to work ‚Äî but how they actually operate when participation is thin and institutional memory is fragile. Fire district commissioners, secretaries, and treasurers are responsible for: Setting and managing a dedicated fire protection tax rate Overseeing contracts, equipment purchases, and audits Maintaining compliance with procurement thresholds, public notice laws, and state reporting requirements Ensuring emergency services remain functional ‚Äî even when volunteer capacity is strained On paper, some of this looks minimal. One meeting per month. About an hour. In reality, that is the bare procedural minimum. As Kacy explained during the interview, the moment someone actually tries to understand the system, fix long-standing problems, or modernize basic operations, the time commitment explodes. He described periods where he was at the firehouse until 2 a.m. night after night ‚Äî not because anyone demanded it, but because unresolved issues don‚Äôt fix themselves. This is not corruption. It‚Äôs not laziness. It‚Äôs not malice. It‚Äôs a system quietly running on unpriced labor and invisible responsibility. The uncomfortable math no one talks about One of the most striking revelations from the interview was compensation ‚Äî not as a scandal, but as a lens. In Laurel Lake‚Äôs Fire District #3, commissioners are compensated $3,400 per year. That figure is higher than in many other districts, and Kacy openly acknowledged it may warrant future discussion. But here‚Äôs the math ‚Äî and it matters: Meeting the formal minimum requirements of the role ~10 hours per year (12 meetings, imperfect attendance) ‚Üí ~$340 per hour Doing more than just attending the monthly meetings ~35 hours per year ‚Üí ~$97 per hour Working through legacy issues, modernization needs, and institutional learning 100+ hours per year ‚Üí ~$34 per hour In a county that routinely describes itself as economically strained ‚Äî and where people frequently say they can‚Äôt find meaningful work ‚Äî this should raise eyebrows. Two seats will be open in district 3. Who needs paid work? Not because the compensation is excessive. But because Kacy says recruitment is very difficult. That contradiction is the point. Other districts may compensate less, or only pay the secretary and treasurer. That variation is real and important. But Laurel Lake provides something rare in public governance: a clear case study. If even this structure struggles to attract candidates and voters, then the problem is not simply money. It‚Äôs probably also visibility, design, and culture. Why participation is the only honest stress test Fire districts are optional under New Jersey law. Municipalities can provide fire services directly. Fire districts exist because residents once chose a model that emphasized: Dedicated funding Local control Elected oversight That choice only makes sense if people remain involved. When elections draw 20‚Äì30 votes per year, the system does not collapse ‚Äî but it does quietly hollow out. Elections become procedural clearance. Authority becomes inherited by default. Oversight becomes theoretical. At that point, debates about consolidation, restructuring, or eliminating elections entirely start to sound less radical ‚Äî not because people want them, but because the current model is no longer being exercised as designed. Before we jump to those conclusions, there is a simpler, more honest test available: Try participation first. So what do we do now? With February elections approaching, the most constructive path forward is not reform ‚Äî it‚Äôs making the elections visible enough to see what happens. That means: Talking openly about what fire districts are Explaining what commissioners actually do Making candidacy legible and approachable Removing the mystique and silence that surrounds the process In Laurel Lake specifically, the message is almost blunt: If you care about your community, and you have the time, this is real civic work ‚Äî and it is not unpaid charity. For other districts, where compensation may be lower or nonexistent, the question becomes even sharper: Why are we governing essential emergency services through elections that almost no one engages with? That question cannot be answered in the abstract. It can only be answered by trying to engage ‚Äî and seeing where it fails. ‚ÄúMake Firefighting Sexy Again‚Äù (seriously) The phrase started half-jokingly during the interview, but it stuck for a reason. Fire protection is one of the few services everyone assumes will work flawlessly, even though most of the work happens out of sight ‚Äî waiting, training, maintaining readiness, responding at inconvenient hours. If communities want reliable emergency services, they should not simultaneously treat the governance of those services as an afterthought. Making firefighting ‚Äúsexy‚Äù doesn‚Äôt mean glorification. It means visibility, respect, and civic seriousness. It means acknowledging that democracy doesn‚Äôt sustain itself through paperwork and elections alone ‚Äî but through participation that gives those structures meaning. Closing thought The question is no longer whether fire district elections matter. They clearly do. The question is whether we are willing, even briefly, to treat them as if they matter ‚Äî and see what that reveals. In the coming months, I‚Äôll continue documenting: how these elections are communicated, who runs, who votes, and what happens when sunlight is applied. If participation rises, the system may justify itself. If it doesn‚Äôt, we‚Äôll at least have evidence ‚Äî not assumptions ‚Äî to guide what comes next. Either way, silence is no longer an acceptable default.
