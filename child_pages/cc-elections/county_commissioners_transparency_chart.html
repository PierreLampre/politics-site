Quick Transparency Breakdown

Two seats are open on the Cumberland County Board of Commissioners. While Republican incumbents Doug Albrecht and Victoria Lods are running unopposed in the primary, they continue to benefit from regular public visibility through bi-weekly GOP breakfasts. This visibility, paired with their incumbency, gives them an edge—not just in name recognition but in familiarity with ongoing county issues. However, as we’ve learned over two years of pursuing comprehensive campaign coverage, there are many underexplored layers in county governance that deserve more clarity during election season.

Later today, we are scheduled to participate in a radio interview with Commissioner Albrecht, and potentially with Commissioner Lods, though her participation has not yet been confirmed. While this is a welcome step toward transparency, the limited one-hour format and uncertainty around Lods’ attendance complicate our ability to prepare effectively or ask in-depth follow-ups. Given the scarcity of prior access and the significance of this race, this may well be the only opportunity to directly engage both incumbents in a meaningful conversation about their campaign messaging.

On the Democratic side, the three candidates—John Capizola, Yolanda Balicki, and LaRae Smith—are in various stages of engagement. Capizola and Balicki initially agreed to complete a questionnaire and sit for an interview, but have yet to follow through. In contrast, Smith has released a clear platform, participated in a full interview, and demonstrated continued commitment to transparency, despite being unaffiliated with the local party’s limited digital infrastructure.

Notably, the Cumberland County Democratic Party has yet to post any 2025 event listings or candidate visibility efforts online—not even for their endorsed slate. This lack of coordination risks leaving voters in the dark just weeks before the primary. Regardless of party affiliation, basic campaign information should be easy to find in 2025—not hidden behind inconsistent communication or outdated outreach practices.

As a reminder, our outreach process includes mailed letters and emails sent to candidate-provided addresses through the County Clerk, regular attendance at campaign events and political meetings, online searches for each candidate, and ongoing efforts to encourage transparency at the county level. We have also urged state lawmakers to reform election law to require counties to centralize and distribute this kind of voter information themselves.
