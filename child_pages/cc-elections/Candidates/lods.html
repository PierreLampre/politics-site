quick summary

Victoria Lods

Victoria Groetsch-Lods is a first-term Cumberland County Commissioner with a background spanning communications, education, agriculture, and nonprofit leadership. She has served as a high school teacher, chief of staff in the State Senate, and the first executive director of Cumberland County Habitat for Humanity. Active on her family’s fifth-generation farm, she brings a direct perspective on the challenges facing agriculture and frequently emphasizes the importance of meeting people “where they are” to ensure programs and resources are accessible.

Her current agenda highlights agriculture advocacy (connecting farmers with state programs and Rutgers partnerships), education and workforce development (expanding CCTECH seats and trade pathways), addiction and recovery awareness (with a focus on parent outreach), and responsible growth projects like the Nabb Avenue extension. She also stresses that recreation, open space, and health are essential to making Cumberland more attractive for employers and families.

Recent updates from her tenure include maintaining stable county taxes for two years and lowering the tax rate by two cents in 2025, spearheading a seminar to assist veterans in navigating benefits, and supporting the launch of NJ Transit’s new Vineland Commuter Express to connect residents with job opportunities in Atlantic City.

Lods is a consistent presence at civic events and county functions and has been more accessible than many peers in engaging with local press and interviews. She has increasingly outlined concrete initiatives and accomplishments, offering voters a clearer picture of her priorities when compared to many other candidates throughout the county.

Rating Explanation

Victoria Lods

Across Cumberland County, candidate communication often remains inconsistent—some relying on scattered social media posts, others sticking to broad slogans, and many avoiding direct press engagement altogether. In that environment, the burden on voters to piece together meaningful information is unreasonably high, and the lack of standardized, centralized campaign expectations only reinforces the gap.

Against this backdrop, Commissioner Victoria Groetsch-Lods has shown clear progress this election season. She has not only participated in a structured, in-depth interview that separated her background from her agenda, but also provided detailed responses on agriculture, education, addiction, and infrastructure in ways that break from the county’s usual “surface-level” trend. These efforts make her more accessible and transparent than many peers and demonstrate what is possible when candidates engage with voters through substantive formats.

Her trajectory continues to trend upward as she remains consistently present at public events and open to dialogue with local press. While full-spectrum transparency would benefit from a countywide system that centralizes and standardizes campaign information, Lods has built a credible foundation that sets her apart within the current status quo. If other candidates followed suit—or if the county established clearer expectations for all—voters would have a much easier time making informed decisions. For now, her approach signals a constructive step forward, both for her own campaign and for what transparency could look like across Cumberland County.

Insight Interview With Commissioner Lods



📋 Click here for full interview summary 📋

In this 60-minute interview, Cumberland County Commissioner Victoria Lods sits down with former Freeholder Jim Quinn at the Queen Diner in Millville. The first 30 minutes could be seen as a somewhat informal 'meet the candidate' section as they discuss her personal and professional background, where the second half shifts to focus on a couple of major county-level issues and a long-term development goal.

County Jail Reform and Regional Collaboration:
Lods offers a detailed update on the long-delayed county jail project. While a 2019 plan to build a new jail was shelved, the county is now exploring a joint facility with Camden County—possibly including Gloucester and Salem as well. In the interim, Camden will house Cumberland inmates, replacing the costly and inefficient use of Hudson County facilities.

Broadband Expansion and Fiscal Responsibility:
Lods explains the trajectory of the federally funded Middle Mile broadband initiative. Originally led by Cumberland County, the project has experienced complications and unexpected costs. A pending vote may shift leadership of the grant to Salem County, allowing the project to move forward without additional taxpayer burden while still serving Cumberland residents.

Economic Development through Bayshore Promotion:
In her broader agenda, Lods emphasizes marketing the Bayshore area—kayaking, bird trails, scenic byways, and local dining—as a tourism and quality-of-life attraction. Part of the goal is to entice outside businesses and residents by building a lifestyle-friendly brand for Cumberland County, boosting long-term economic prospects.

Final Thoughts:
Throughout the interview, Lods presents herself as a pragmatic coalition-builder who values cross-county collaboration, infrastructure modernization, and community vitality. Whether you're a casual viewer or closely tracking county affairs, this conversation offers a direct look at her leadership style and priorities ahead of the 2025 election.

Agenda

  • Agriculture: Advocate, Modernize, and “Meet People Where They Are”

    Drawing on her fifth-generation farming background, Lods aims to stabilize and grow local agriculture by: (1) acting as a county-level conduit between farmers and Trenton (alerts, grants, and program access); (2) strengthening Rutgers/Extension partnerships (e.g., on-site packaging/processing options and crop modernization); (3) practicing true “meeting people where they are” outreach (avoid 9 a.m. visits mid-harvest; bring info to auctions/meetups); (4) advocating for incentives that address the cost–price squeeze driving family farms out of business.


  • Education & Workforce Pathways

    As a high school teacher and commissioner liaison, Lods emphasizes adapting instruction to today’s tech realities, expanding CCTECH seats (Cumberland County Technical Education Center), and aligning districts with RCSJ and union apprenticeships. Priority: build a skilled workforce pipeline so employers see Cumberland County as a place to hire and stay.


  • Healthy Communities & Recreation

    To attract businesses and retain families, Lods supports rapidly standing up a county recreation function (a gap she identified), linking open space, health, and recreation to talent attraction and long-term economic development.


  • Addiction & Parent Awareness

    Treat addiction as a countywide health priority: promote overdose spike alerts, expand outreach at back-to-school events, and equip parents with practical knowledge (vapes, edibles, hidden paraphernalia). Notes a generational shift—youth are increasingly cautious after witnessing harms in their communities.


  • Nabb Avenue Extension & Responsible Growth

    Back the Nabb Ave extension to add an artery from Route 55 to the airport/industrial area, relieving congestion and unlocking jobs, funded via a four-pronged approach (federal, state, county, municipal). Emphasis on planning, sequencing, and fiscal stewardship.

Issues

  • Press, Transparency, and Civic Communication

    With a journalism/PR background, Lods underscores that robust communication is core to governing. She supports rebuilding public-facing information flows (press, forums, liaison reports) and expects candidates/officials to engage substantively with voters and local media.


  • Agricultural Margin Squeeze

    Input costs rise while farmgate prices lag. Lods argues for targeted state and federal incentives, plus value-add options (pack/freezing, crop shifts) to improve margins, and county-facilitated access to programs.


  • Education Performance & CTE Expansion

    Acknowledges local performance concerns; focuses on CCTECH growth, early college pathways, and district–employer alignment to raise outcomes and meet labor demand.


  • Addiction Services & Capacity

    Supports Human Services/Prosecutor outreach and alerts; notes the Seabrook House closure removed regional rehab capacity, reinforcing the need for coordinated treatment access and prevention.



Accomplishments

  • Farmer Outreach & Program Access

    Helped organize/participate in town-hall style sessions connecting growers with available programs; advanced a “bring it to them” model (auctions, on-site meetings) so time-constrained farmers can actually apply and benefit.


  • CCTECH Seat Expansion & Liaison Work

    As liaison for RCSJ, CCTECH, and K-12 districts, supported the successful pursuit of funding to expand CCT seats and strengthen school–college–union pipelines into the trades and advanced manufacturing.


  • Recreation Function: From Gap to Build-Out

    Identified that Cumberland was one of the few counties without a recreation department/board; pushed to move from recognition to rapid implementation to support health, quality of life, and employer attraction.


  • Nabb Avenue: Planning & Funding Path

    Backed engineering/planning work and a multi-level funding strategy (federal/state/county/municipal) to keep the project viable while protecting taxpayers.



Comparison to other candidates

  • Substance Over Slogans

    Lods emphasizes concrete liaison work (farm programs, CTE seats, recreation build-out) and collaborative planning, contrasting with platforms that remain vague or light on implementation detail.


  • Transparency & Press Engagement

    She consistently engages in public interviews and expects officials/candidates to communicate openly; positions communication as a job requirement, not a campaign choice.


  • Practical Advocacy vs. Over-Promising

    Frames the county’s role realistically—conduit, coordinator, and advocate—rather than promising fixes outside county authority. Supports four-corner funding for major projects instead of single-source pledges that can stall.



Background and Experience

  • Professional & Educational

    B.A. in Communications/Journalism (Rowan); educator in Bridgeton and Millville; earlier service as Chief of Staff to former State Senator Nicholas Asselta; first Executive Director of Cumberland County Habitat for Humanity.


  • Boards & Civic Roles

    Member, Vineland Zoning Board; Cumberland County Board of Taxation; local NAACP; active on family farm “Victoria Farms,” donating produce to local families.


  • Approach to Governance

    Growth-mindset problem solver; prioritizes communication, coalition building, and responsible, planned development. Comfortable seeking expertise and collaborating across municipal, county, and state levels.
